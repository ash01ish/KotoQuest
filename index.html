Day 1: Hiragana Foundation Phonetics

Welcome to the game. Japanese uses three writing systems: Hiragana (phonetic letters for native words), Katakana (phonetic letters for foreign loanwords), and Kanji (Chinese characters that stand for whole words or ideas). Today is all Hiragana.

The Five Base Vowels (a, i, u, e, o)

Almost every Japanese sound contains one of these vowels — the exceptions are ん (n) and the small っ pause. Pronounce them like this:

  • あ (a) - "ah" as in father
  • い (i) - "ee" as in feet
  • う (u) - "oo" as in boot
  • え (e) - "eh" as in red
  • お (o) - "oh" as in boat

Consonant Grouping System

All other characters are formed by pairing consonants (K, S, T, N, H, M, Y, R, W) with the base vowels. For example, the K-row is: か (ka), き (ki), く (ku), け (ke), こ (ko).

Day 2: Katakana & Sound Modifiers Phonetics Part II

Today you add Katakana, plus the marks that turn base sounds into voiced and combined ones.

1. Katakana (カタカナ)

Katakana represents the exact same sounds as Hiragana but uses sharp, straight strokes. It is used for foreign names, places, and loanwords (e.g., カメラ - kamera for camera).

2. Dakuon & Handakuon (Sound Twists)

By adding quotation marks (dakuten ゛) or small circles (handakuten ゜) to the upper right of a hiragana or katakana, you change the consonant sound:

  • K-row (か) + ゛ = G-row (が - ga)
  • S-row (さ) + ゛ = Z-row (ざ - za)
  • T-row (た) + ゛ = D-row (だ - da)
  • H-row (は) + ゛ = B-row (ば - ba)
  • H-row (は) + ゜ = P-row (ぱ - pa)
3. Yōon (Combined Sounds) & Sokuon (Double Consonants)

We combine Hiragana from the 'i' column (ki, shi, chi, etc.) with small versions of ゃ (ya), ゅ (yu), ょ (yo) to make sounds like きゃ (kya) or しゅ (shu).

A small っ (tsu) creates a brief double consonant pause in speech (e.g., さっき - sakki: a moment ago).

Day 4: Particles & Sentence Structure Grammar

Now for how sentences fit together. English is Subject-Verb-Object ("I eat sushi"). Japanese is Subject-Object-Verb ("I sushi eat").

1. Word Order Structure

English: I read books.

Japanese: わたしは ほんを よみます (Watashi wa hon o yomimasu) → I [Topic] Book [Object] Read.

2. Critical Particles (助詞)

Particles are small words added after nouns to indicate their grammatical role:

Particle Function Example
は (wa) Marks the main topic of the sentence. (Written "ha" but spoken "wa".) わたしは... (watashi wa... — As for me...)
を (o) Marks the direct object receiving the action. (Written "wo" but spoken "o".) ほんをよむ (hon o yomu — Read a book)
に (ni) Marks time or destination (to/at). にほんにいく (nihon ni iku — Go to Japan)
で (de) Marks the location of an action, or the tool used (by/at). レストランでたべる (resutoran de taberu — Eat at a restaurant)
の (no) Shows possession (like apostrophe-s in English). わたしのねこ (watashi no neko — My cat)

Day 5: Verbs & Conjugation Grammar Part II

Today we learn Japanese verbs and how to conjugate them into formal polite levels. Japanese conjugation is incredibly regular - no changing by subject person (I, you, they all conjugate exactly the same)!

1. Verb Groups

All Japanese verbs in their dictionary (base) form end in a 'u' sound. They fall into three groups:

  • Ru-verbs (Group 2): End in -iru or -eru. (e.g., たべる - taberu: to eat, みる - miru: to watch).
  • U-verbs (Group 1): End in other -u sounds. (e.g., のむ - nomu: to drink, いく - iku: to go, はなす - hanasu: to speak).
  • Watch out: not every -iru/-eru verb is a ru-verb — a handful are u-verbs you just memorize: かえる (kaeru — to return), はしる (hashiru — to run), はいる (hairu — to enter), きる (kiru — to cut), いる (iru — to need).
  • Irregular Verbs (Group 3): Only two! する (suru: to do) and くる (kuru: to come).
2. The Polite (~masu) Form Conjugation

To talk politely, change verbs to the ~ます (~masu) suffix. Conjugation formulas:

Group Dictionary Form Polite Form (Positive) Polite Form (Negative)
Ru-verbs (Drop -ru, add -masu) たべる (taberu) たべます (tabemasu) たべません (tabemasen)
U-verbs (Change 'u' to 'i' vowel, add -masu) のむ (nomu) のみます (nomimasu) のみません (nomimasen)
Irregulars (Fixed change) する (suru) / くる (kuru) します / きます しません / きません
3. Past Tense Polite
  • Past Affirmative: Replace ~ます with ~ました (~mashita). E.g., たべました (tabemashita — I ate).
  • Past Negative: Replace ~ます with ~ませんでした (~masendeshita). E.g., たべませんでした (tabemasendeshita — I did not eat).

N4·1: The Te-form (て形) Grammar

The te-form is the connector of Japanese grammar — it links actions and powers requests, permission, and the progressive. Here is how to build it and what it does.

Reminder: る-verbs (Group 2) end in -iru/-eru like たべる (taberu); う-verbs (Group 1) are the rest like のむ (nomu) — covered in N5·5.

1. How to form it
Verb endingChangeExample
る-verbsる → てたべる → たべて (taberu → tabete)
う・つ・る→ ってかう → かって (kau → katte)
む・ぶ・ぬ→ んでのむ → のんで (nomu → nonde)
く→ いてかく → かいて (kaku → kaite)
ぐ→ いでおよぐ → およいで (oyogu → oyoide)
す→ してはなす → はなして (hanasu → hanashite)
Irregular—する→して, くる→きて (suru → shite, kuru → kite)

Exception: いく → いって (iku → itte) (not いいて).

2. What it does
  • Request: ～てください — みてください (mite kudasai — please look)
  • Permission: ～てもいいです — たべてもいいです (tabete mo ii desu — you may eat)
  • Progressive / state: ～ています — よんでいます (yonde imasu — am reading)
  • Prohibition: ～てはいけません — はいってはいけません (haitte wa ikemasen — you must not enter)
  • Linking actions: おきて、たべて、いきます (okite, tabete, ikimasu — I get up, eat, and go)

N4·2: Plain (Casual) Form Grammar

Polite ～ます is for strangers and formal settings. With friends and family, Japanese uses the plain form — and you need it to build most advanced grammar.

Present tense: plain vs polite
MeaningPolitePlain
eatたべます (tabemasu)たべる (taberu)
don't eatたべません (tabemasen)たべない (tabenai)
drinkのみます (nomimasu)のむ (nomu)
don't drinkのみません (nomimasen)のまない (nomanai)
is (a thing)です (desu)だ (da)
The plain negative (～ない)
  • る-verbs: drop る, add ない — みる (miru — to see) → みない (minai)
  • う-verbs: final -u → -a + ない — のむ (nomu — to drink) → のまない (nomanai) (う becomes わ: かう (kau — to buy) → かわない (kawanai))
  • Irregular: する (suru — to do) → しない (shinai), くる (kuru — to come) → こない (konai), ある (aru — to exist) → ない (nai)

Tip: casual speech drops です／か — たべる？ (taberu?) (rising tone) means "gonna eat?"

N4·3: Plain Past & Past Negative Grammar

The plain past (た-form) is built exactly like the te-form — just swap て/で for た/だ. It powers casual speech and grammar like ～たことがある (have done).

Past affirmative (～た)
GroupRuleExample
る-verbsる → たたべる → たべた (taberu → tabeta — eat → ate)
う・つ・る→ ったかう → かった (kau → katta — buy → bought)
む・ぶ・ぬ→ んだのむ → のんだ (nomu → nonda — drink → drank)
く / ぐ→ いた / いだかく → かいた (kaku → kaita — write → wrote)
す→ したはなす → はなした (hanasu → hanashita — speak → spoke)
Irregular—する→した, くる→きた, いく→いった (suru → shita, kuru → kita, iku → itta — do/come/go → did/came/went)
Past negative (～なかった)

Take the plain negative ～ない and change い → かった:

  • たべない (tabenai) → たべなかった (tabenakatta) (didn't eat)
  • いかない (ikanai) → いかなかった (ikanakatta) (didn't go)
  • い-adjectives too: たかい → たかくなかった (takai → takaku nakatta — expensive → wasn't expensive)

N4·5: Giving & Receiving Grammar

Japanese picks the verb based on direction — who gives to whom. Get these three straight and a lot of everyday speech opens up.

The three verbs
  • あげる — I/someone gives outward. わたしは ともだちに プレゼントを あげる. (Watashi wa tomodachi ni purezento o ageru. — I give my friend a present.)
  • くれる — someone gives to me. ともだちが わたしに プレゼントを くれる. (Tomodachi ga watashi ni purezento o kureru. — My friend gives me a present.)
  • もらう — I receive (giver marked に／から). わたしは ともだちに プレゼントを もらう. (Watashi wa tomodachi ni purezento o morau. — I receive a present from my friend.)

Rule of thumb: if the gift comes to you, it is くれる or もらう — never あげる.

N4·6: Potential Form — "can do" Grammar

The potential form means "can / be able to." One twist: the object particle を usually becomes が.

How to form it
GroupRuleExample
る-verbsる → られるたべる (taberu) → たべられる (taberareru) (can eat)
う-verbs-u → -eruのむ → のめる (nomeru) (can drink), かく → かける (kakeru) (can write)
Irregular—する → できる (dekiru) (can do), くる → こられる (korareru) (can come)
The を → が shift
  • にほんごをはなす (nihongo o hanasu — speak Japanese) → にほんごがはなせる (nihongo ga hanaseru — can speak Japanese)
  • かんじがよめる (kanji ga yomeru — can read kanji)
  • Casual speech often drops the ら: たべられる (taberareru) → たべれる (tabereru) ("ra-less" form). This ra-nuki is common in everyday speech, but it's still considered nonstandard in writing and on exams.

N3·1: Conditionals I — ～たら & ～と Grammar

N3 opens up four ways to say "if/when" — ～たら, ～と, ～ば, and ～なら. This lesson covers the first two: the flexible all-purpose ～たら, and ～と, used for automatic or habitual results.

1. Forming ～たら and ～と
FormRuleExample
～たらplain past (た-form) + らのむ → のんだ → のんだら (nomu → nonda → nondara — drink → if/when [you] drink)
～たら (い-adj)～かった + らたかい → たかかったら (takai → takakattara — if [it] is expensive)
～とplain present (dictionary form) + とまがる → まがると (magaru → magaru to — if/when [you] turn)
～と (noun/な-adj)～だ + としずかだ → しずかだと (shizuka da → shizuka da to — if [it's] quiet)
2. たら is flexible, と is not
  • ～たら allows any main clause — requests, invitations, commands: いえにかえったら、でんわしてください (ie ni kaettara, denwa shite kudasai — when you get home, please call)
  • ～と describes automatic or general results — the main clause can't be a request, command, or invitation: はるになると、さくらがさきます (haru ni naru to, sakura ga sakimasu — when spring comes, cherry blossoms bloom)
  • Both can mean "when" for things certain to happen: みぎにまがると、ぎんこうがあります (migi ni magaru to, ginkou ga arimasu — if/when you turn right, there is a bank)
Practice what you learned

Try both conditionals in the Quest Arena, then build your own if/when sentences in the Sentence Builder.

N3·2: Conditionals II — ～ば & ～なら Grammar

Two more conditionals round out the N3 set. ～ば suits hypothetical conditions, proverbs, and negative "musts." ～なら reacts to something the other person already said — "if that's the case..."

1. Forming ～ば
GroupRuleExample
う-verbs-u → -e + ばのむ → のめば (nomu → nomeba — drink → if [you] drink)
る-verbsdrop る + ればたべる → たべれば (taberu → tabereba — eat → if [you] eat)
Irregular—する → すれば, くる → くれば (suru → sureba, kuru → kureba)
い-adjectivesい → ければたかい → たかければ (takai → takakereba — if [it] is expensive)
Any negative～ない → なければたべない → たべなければ (tabenai → tabenakereba — if [you] don't eat)

This negative ば-form is exactly what ～なければならない ("must") is built on.

2. ～なら — reacting to a premise
  • Nouns and な-adjectives attach directly, no だ: げんきなら (genki nara — if [you're] energetic), never げんきだなら.
  • なら answers a topic the other person just raised: A: にほんへいきます。 B: にほんへいくなら、パスポートがひつようです。 (nihon e iku nara, pasupooto ga hitsuyou desu — if you're going to Japan, you'll need a passport)
  • Unlike ～たら/～と/～ば, the outcome can logically come before the なら-clause in time — it's commenting on the premise, not describing a strict sequence.
3. ～ば～ほど — "the more..., the more..."

Repeat the verb: ～ば form + plain form + ほど.

  • たべればたべるほど、ふとります (tabereba taberu hodo, futorimasu — the more you eat, the fatter you get)

N3·3: Passive — ～られる Grammar

The passive voice describes an action done to the subject — and Japanese stretches it further than English, letting a speaker describe being negatively affected by someone else's action entirely.

1. Forming the passive
GroupRuleExample
う-verbs-u → -a + れる (う → わ)のむ → のまれる (nomu → nomareru); かう → かわれる (kau → kawareru)
う-verbs ending in うう → われるかう → かわれる (kau → kawareru — buy → be bought)
る-verbsdrop る + られるたべる → たべられる (taberu → taberareru — eat → be eaten)
Irregular—する → される, くる → こられる (suru → sareru, kuru → korareru)

Note: る-verb passive and potential (N4·6) share the same form — context tells them apart.

2. Direct passive — marking the agent
  • The "by ~" agent is marked with に, or によって for creative/impersonal works.
  • わたしはせんせいにほめられた (watashi wa sensei ni homerareta — I was praised by the teacher)
  • このほんはなつめそうせきによってかかれた (kono hon wa Natsume Souseki ni yotte kakareta — this book was written by Natsume Souseki)
3. The suffering passive (迷惑の受身)
  • Uniquely Japanese: even verbs with no direct object can take the passive to show the speaker was inconvenienced by someone else's action.
  • あめにふられた (ame ni furareta — I got rained on [and it was a bother])
  • ともだちにこられて、べんきょうできなかった (tomodachi ni korarete, benkyou dekinakatta — a friend came over [on me] and I couldn't study)
  • The English translation rarely captures the nuance — think "I suffered from X happening," not a literal translation.

N3·4: Causative & Causative-Passive Grammar

The causative describes making or letting someone do something. Combine it with the passive and you get the causative-passive: being made to do something you didn't want to.

1. Forming the causative (～せる／させる)
GroupRuleExample
う-verbs-u → -a + せる (う → わ)のむ → のませる (nomu → nomaseru); かう → かわせる (kau → kawaseru)
る-verbsdrop る + させるたべる → たべさせる (taberu → tabesaseru — eat → make/let [someone] eat)
Irregular—する → させる, くる → こさせる (suru → saseru, kuru → kosaseru)
2. "Make" vs "let" — and who gets に or を
  • はははこどもにやさいをたべさせた (haha wa kodomo ni yasai o tabesaseta — the mother made/had the child eat vegetables) — transitive verb: the person forced takes に.
  • せんせいはがくせいをはやくかえらせた (sensei wa gakusei o hayaku kaeraseta — the teacher made the students go home early) — intransitive verb かえる: the person forced takes を.
  • Context and tone decide "make" (forceful) vs "let" (permissive): どうぞやすませてください (douzo yasumasete kudasai — please let me rest) is clearly permissive.
3. The causative-passive (～させられる) — being made to do it
  • Build it from the causative stem + られる: たべさせる → たべさせられる (tabesaseru → tabesaserareru — was made to eat).
  • う-verbs often contract ～せられる to ～される: のませられる → のまされる (nomaserareru → nomasareru) — both forms are correct; the contraction is more common in speech.
  • わたしはははにやさいをたべさせられた (watashi wa haha ni yasai o tabesaserareta — I was made to eat vegetables by my mother [and I didn't want to])

N3·5: Change & Decisions Grammar

These four patterns look alike but split along two lines: なる (become) describes a change that happens on its own, while する (do) describes a deliberate action — and よう (a state/ability) contrasts with こと (a fact/decision).

1. ～ようになる — a change of state or ability
  • potential/dictionary form + ようになる — a gradual, natural change over time.
  • かんじがよめるようになった (kanji ga yomeru you ni natta — I've come to be able to read kanji [I couldn't before])
  • まいにちにほんごをはなすようになりました (mainichi nihongo o hanasu you ni narimashita — I've come to speak Japanese every day)
2. ～ことになる — decided by others, or an arrangement
  • dictionary/ない-form + ことになる — announces a decision made by circumstances, a group, or someone else — not the speaker personally.
  • らいげつ、とうきょうにてんきんすることになった (raigetsu, Tokyo ni tenkin suru koto ni natta — it's been decided that I'll transfer to Tokyo next month)
3. ～ようにする／～ことにする — the deliberate versions
  • ～ようにする: dictionary/ない-form + ようにする — an ongoing conscious effort or habit. まいにちうんどうするようにしています (mainichi undou suru you ni shiteimasu — I make it a point to exercise every day)
  • ～ことにする: dictionary/ない-form + ことにする — a one-time personal decision, made by the speaker's own will. かいしゃをやめることにした (kaisha o yameru koto ni shita — I've decided to quit the company)

N3·6: Hearsay & Appearance Grammar

Four ways to say "seems" or "I hear that" — and one of them, そうだ, means two completely different things depending on how it attaches. Get the attachment rule right and the rest falls into place.

1. そうだ — hearsay vs appearance (the trap)
MeaningAttaches toExample
Hearsay (伝聞) — "I heard that..."plain form, any tense (noun/な-adj + だ)あしたはあめだそうです (ashita wa ame da sou desu — I heard it will rain tomorrow)
Appearance (様態) — "looks/seems like..."verb ます-stem / adjective stem (no い, no だ)あめがふりそうです (ame ga furisou desu — it looks like it's going to rain)
  • Hearsay そうだ never drops だ before nouns/な-adjectives: これはぺんだそうです (kore wa pen da sou desu — I heard this is a pen).
  • Appearance そうだ drops だ entirely and attaches straight to the stem: おいしい → おいしそう (oishii → oishisou — looks delicious); げんき → げんきそう (genki → genkisou — looks well).
  • Two irregular appearance stems: いい → よさそう (ii → yosasou, not iisou); ない → なさそう (nai → nasasou).
2. らしい — hearsay with distance, or "typical of"
  • plain form (noun without だ) + らしい — reports something heard, usually with more distance/uncertainty than そうだ, often from general rumor rather than a direct source.
  • たなかさんはらいげつけっこんするらしいです (Tanaka-san wa raigetsu kekkon suru rashii desu — I hear Tanaka is getting married next month)
  • Also means "typical of / just like": おとこらしい (otoko rashii — manly, like a "real man").
3. みたい & ようだ — inference from what you see
  • Both mean "seems/looks like," based on observation or comparison — みたい is casual, ようだ is more formal/written.
  • Nouns/な-adjectives: みたい attaches directly (あめみたい); ようだ needs の／な (あめのようだ, げんきなようだ).
  • あのひとはがくせいみたいです (ano hito wa gakusei mitai desu — that person seems to be a student)
  • かれはびょうきのようだ (kare wa byouki no you da — he seems to be sick)

N3·7: Te-form Extensions Grammar

The te-form keeps earning its keep at N3 — attach おく, しまう, ある, or みる to it and you unlock four more everyday nuances.

1. ～ておく — do it in advance, or leave it as is
  • Prepares something ahead of time, or leaves something as-is for a future benefit.
  • ビールをかっておく (biiru o katte oku — buy beer in advance [for the party later])
  • Casual speech contracts ～ておく to ～とく: かっておく → かっとく (katte oku → kattoku).
2. ～てしまう — completion, or "oops"
  • Emphasizes something is finished completely, often with a nuance of regret or unintentional action.
  • しゅくだいをやってしまった (shukudai o yatte shimatta — I went and finished the homework)
  • Casual contraction: ～てしまう → ～ちゃう (or ～でしまう → ～じゃう): たべてしまう → たべちゃう (tabete shimau → tabechau).
3. ～てある vs ～ている, and ～てみる — "try it"
  • ～てある (transitive verb + ある): a state left behind on purpose by someone. まどがあけてある (mado ga akete aru — the window has [intentionally] been left open)
  • Compare ～ている: まどがあいている (mado ga aiteiru — the window is open) — a simple state, no emphasis on who left it that way or why.
  • ～てみる: "try doing" something to see what happens. たべてみる (tabete miru — try eating [it])

N3·8: Keigo I — Honorific & Humble Verbs Grammar

敬語 (keigo) is Japan's system of respectful speech. 尊敬語 (honorific) elevates someone else's action; 謙譲語 (humble) lowers your own action to show respect to the listener. Some of the most common verbs have entirely irregular keigo forms — learn these first.

1. Plain → honorific → humble
PlainHonorific (尊敬語)Humble (謙譲語)
いる (iru — to be)いらっしゃる (irassharu)おる (oru)
いく／くる (iku/kuru — go/come)いらっしゃる (irassharu)まいる (mairu)
いう (iu — say)おっしゃる (ossharu)もうす (mousu)
たべる／のむ (taberu/nomu — eat/drink)めしあがる (meshiagaru)いただく (itadaku)
する (suru — do)なさる (nasaru)いたす (itasu)

Honorific verbs elevate other people's actions (a teacher, a customer, your boss); humble verbs lower your own actions to show the listener respect. Never mix the two for the same person.

2. Watch the ます-form — four irregulars
  • いらっしゃる, おっしゃる, なさる, and くださる all take ～います, not ～ります: いらっしゃる → いらっしゃいます (irassharu → irasshaimasu), おっしゃる → おっしゃいます (ossharu → osshaimasu).
  • せんせいはいついらっしゃいますか (sensei wa itsu irasshaimasu ka — when will the teacher arrive?)
  • しゃちょうはなんとおっしゃいましたか (shachou wa nan to osshaimashita ka — what did the president say?)
3. Humble in action — lowering yourself
  • たなかともうします (Tanaka to moushimasu — I am called Tanaka [humble self-introduction])
  • わたしはあしたまいります (watashi wa ashita mairimasu — I will go/come tomorrow [humble])
  • どうぞめしあがってください (douzo meshiagatte kudasai — please go ahead and eat [honorific, said to a guest])

N2·1: ～ものだ Family Grammar

もの ("thing") turns into three very different endings here: ものだ voices a general truth, a nostalgic memory, or a gentle scolding; ものの concedes a fact before pivoting to something contrary; ものだから leans on a reason to excuse what just happened.

1. ものだ — three moods, one ending
UseRuleExample
General truthplain form + ものだひとはだれでもしっぱいするものだ (hito wa dare demo shippai suru mono da — everyone makes mistakes, that's just how it is)
Nostalgia (past habit)た-form + ものだこどものころ、よくこのかわでおよいだものだ (kodomo no koro, yoku kono kawa de oyoida mono da — when I was a kid, I used to swim in this river often)
Mild command ("should")plain form + ものだあいさつはきちんとするものだ (aisatsu wa kichinto suru mono da — you should greet people properly)
2. ものの — although, but then...
  • Plain form (any tense) + ものの concedes a fact, then states an unexpected or disappointing result — more formal/written than けれども.
  • にほんごをさんねんべんきょうしたものの、まだうまくはなせない (nihongo o sannen benkyou shita mono no, mada umaku hanasenai — although I've studied Japanese for three years, I still can't speak it well)
  • あたまではわかっているものの、なかなかじっこうできない (atama de wa wakatteiru mono no, nakanaka jikkou dekinai — although I understand it in my head, I can't easily put it into practice)
3. ものだから — the excuse-reason
  • Plain form + ものだから gives a reason that doubles as an excuse, usually followed by an apology or explanation — common in spoken Japanese.
  • でんしゃがおくれたものだから、ちこくしてしまいました (densha ga okureta mono da kara, chikoku shite shimaimashita — the train was late, so [that's why] I ended up being late)
  • Casual speech contracts it: ものだから → もんだから (mono da kara → mon da kara).

N2·2: ～わけだ Family Grammar

わけ ("reason/circumstance") builds three N2 staples. わけだ draws the natural conclusion — "no wonder, so that means..." わけではない softens a blanket statement into "it's not necessarily..." and わけにはいかない says a social or moral obligation rules an action out.

1. わけだ — no wonder / so that means
  • Plain form + わけだ states a conclusion that logically follows from what was just said or observed.
  • さんねんかんにほんにすんでいた。だから、にほんごがじょうずなわけだ (san-nen kan nihon ni sundeita. dakara, nihongo ga jouzu na wake da — they lived in Japan for three years. No wonder their Japanese is good)
  • どうりでさむいわけだ、ゆきがふっている (douri de samui wake da, yuki ga futteiru — no wonder it's cold, it's snowing)
2. わけではない — it's not that... / not necessarily
  • Plain form + わけではない partially denies an assumption without rejecting it outright.
  • きらいなわけではないが、あまりたべたくない (kirai na wake de wa nai ga, amari tabetakunai — it's not that I dislike it, but I don't really want to eat it)
  • ぜんぶわかったわけではない (zenbu wakatta wake de wa nai — it's not that I understood everything)
3. わけにはいかない — can't afford to / can't just
  • Dictionary form + わけにはいかない: a social, moral, or circumstantial obligation blocks an action, even if you'd like to do it.
  • あしたはしけんがあるから、きょうはあそぶわけにはいかない (ashita wa shiken ga aru kara, kyou wa asobu wake ni wa ikanai — I have an exam tomorrow, so I can't just go play today)
  • The negative twist, ないわけにはいかない ("can't afford not to"), gets its own spotlight next lesson.

N2·3: Obligation & Compulsion Grammar

Three heavier ways to say "I have no choice." ～ざるを得ない is reluctant compulsion from circumstances beyond your control. ～ずにはいられない is an emotional urge too strong to suppress. ～ないわけにはいかない is a social or moral duty you can't skip.

1. Forming ～ざるを得ない (and ～ずにはいられない)
GroupRuleExample
う-verbsない-form stem + ざるを得ないのむ → のまない → のまざるをえない (nomu → nomanai → nomazaru o enai — drink → have no choice but to drink)
る-verbsない-form stem + ざるを得ないやめる → やめない → やめざるをえない (yameru → yamenai → yamezaru o enai — quit → have no choice but to quit)
Irregular するせざるを得ない, not さざるを得ないする → せざるをえない (suru → sezaru o enai)

～ずにはいられない is built the same way — swap ざるを得ない for ずにはいられない (irregular する → せずにはいられない, same せ as above).

2. ～ざるを得ない in action — reluctant compulsion
  • きゅうりょうがやすいので、やめざるをえない (kyuuryou ga yasui node, yamezaru o enai — the pay is low, so I have no choice but to quit)
  • じょうしにいわれたら、ざんぎょうせざるをえない (joushi ni iwaretara, zangyou sezaru o enai — if the boss tells you to, you have no choice but to work overtime)
3. ～ずにはいられない vs ～ないわけにはいかない
  • ～ずにはいられない is an emotional impulse too strong to resist: あのえいがをみると、なかずにはいられない (ano eiga o miru to, nakazu ni wa irarenai — when I watch that movie, I can't help but cry)
  • ～ないわけにはいかない (ない-form + わけにはいかない) is a social or moral duty you can't skip: せんせいによばれたら、いかないわけにはいかない (sensei ni yobaretara, ikanai wake ni wa ikanai — if the teacher calls you, you can't not go)
  • The difference: ざるを得ない points at "no realistic alternative"; ないわけにはいかない points at "duty says I must."

N2·4: Concessives Grammar

Three ways to flag a mismatch between expectation and reality. にもかかわらず is the strongest "despite/even though." にしては judges something against what its category should imply. わりに(は) says a result is disproportionate to the given condition — a milder, more neutral cousin of にしては.

1. にもかかわらず — despite / in spite of
  • Plain form / noun + にもかかわらず states a fact, then a surprising or contrary result — formal, common in writing.
  • あめにもかかわらず、おおぜいのひとがきた (ame ni mo kakawarazu, oozei no hito ga kita — despite the rain, a lot of people came)
  • いっしょうけんめいべんきょうしたにもかかわらず、しけんにおちてしまった (isshoukenmei benkyou shita ni mo kakawarazu, shiken ni ochite shimatta — despite studying hard, I failed the exam)
2. にしては — for a... / considering (surprised tone)
  • Noun / plain form + にしては judges someone or something against the standard implied by a category: "for a ~, this is surprising."
  • こどもにしては、よくかんじをしっている (kodomo ni shite wa, yoku kanji o shitteiru — for a kid, they know a lot of kanji)
  • はじめてにしては、じょうずにできた (hajimete ni shite wa, jouzu ni dekita — for a first try, it went well)
3. わりに(は) — relatively / considering (neutral tone)
  • Noun (+の) / な-adjective (+な) / plain form + わりに(は): the result doesn't match what the condition would normally predict — unlike にしては, no built-in "standard" is required.
  • このみせはねだんがたかいわりには、あまりおいしくない (kono mise wa nedan ga takai wari ni wa, amari oishikunai — considering how expensive this restaurant is, it's not very tasty)
  • ねんれいのわりに、わかくみえる (nenrei no wari ni, wakaku mieru — they look young for their age)

N2·5: Tendencies Grammar

Three suffixes for "leaning toward" a state without fully being it. ～がち is a recurring pattern, usually unwanted. ～気味 (ぎみ) is a slight, often physical, tendency happening right now. ～っぽい paints something as "-ish" — resembling a quality or type.

1. ～がち — prone to / tends to
  • ます-stem or noun + がち marks a recurring tendency, usually toward something undesirable.
  • かれはやくそくをわすれがちだ (kare wa yakusoku o wasuregachi da — he tends to forget his promises)
  • このごろ、くもりがちのてんきがつづいている (kono goro, kumorigachi no tenki ga tsuzuiteiru — lately, the weather has tended to be cloudy)
  • Common set phrase: びょうきがち (byoukigachi — sickly, prone to getting ill).
2. ～気味 (ぎみ) — a slight sense of / a bit
  • ます-stem or noun + 気味: a slight current tendency or feeling, milder and more physical/mental than がち.
  • さいきん、つかれぎみだ (saikin, tsukaregimi da — I've been feeling a bit tired lately)
  • かぜぎみなので、きょうははやくねます (kazegimi na node, kyou wa hayaku nemasu — I'm feeling a bit like I have a cold, so I'll go to bed early today)
3. ～っぽい — -ish / looks or acts like
  • い-adjective stem / noun / ます-stem + っぽい gives an impression of a quality, often slightly negative or casual.
  • わすれっぽいひとですね (wasureppoi hito desu ne — you're a forgetful person, aren't you)
  • かのじょはこどもっぽい (kanojo wa kodomoppoi — she's childish)
  • みずっぽいスープ (mizuppoi suupu — watery soup)

N2·6: Correlated Change Grammar

All three describe two things changing together — "as X changes, Y changes" — and they overlap heavily. But each leans a different way: につれて is gradual and natural, に伴って highlights one change bringing about another, and に従って can also mean "in accordance with" a rule.

1. Forming and comparing all three
PatternNuanceExample
～につれてgradual, parallel natural changeとしをとるにつれて、からだがよわくなる (toshi o toru ni tsurete, karada ga yowaku naru — as one ages, the body grows weaker)
～に伴ってone change brings/accompanies another, often larger-scaleじんこうのぞうかにともなって、じゅうたくぶそくがしんこくかしている (jinkou no zouka ni tomonatte, juutaku busoku ga shinkokuka shiteiru — along with population growth, the housing shortage is worsening)
～に従ってfollowing a rule/instruction, or gradual progressionきそくにしたがってこうどうしてください (kisoku ni shitagatte koudou shite kudasai — please act in accordance with the rules)
2. Where they split
  • For a purely gradual, parallel change, all three can often substitute for one another: ぶっかがあがるにつれて、せいかつがくるしくなる (bukka ga agaru ni tsurete, seikatsu ga kurushiku naru — as prices rise, life gets harder) — に伴って or に従って would work here too with little change in meaning.
  • Only に伴って naturally attaches to event nouns describing an accompanying consequence: たいふうのせっきんにともなって、でんしゃがとまった (taifuu no sekkin ni tomonatte, densha ga tomatta — with the typhoon's approach, trains stopped)
  • Only に従って extends naturally to "in accordance with" a rule, order, or instruction — not just gradual change.

N2·7: Written Japanese Grammar

Formal written Japanese — newspapers, reports, academic papers — swaps the polite です／ます register for the plain・である style, and leans on a handful of grammar patterns rarely heard in casual speech.

1. である style — the written-report register
Casual/polite endingWritten (である) equivalent

2. により and における — cause and context, formally
  • ～により (formal for によって／で) marks a cause, means, or method: たいふうにより、おおくのいえがこわれた (taifuu ni yori, ooku no ie ga kowareta — due to the typhoon, many houses were destroyed)

  • あたらしいほうりつをめぐって、はげしいぎろんがつづいている (atarashii houritsu o megutte, hageshii giron ga tsuzuiteiru — fierce debate continues over the new law)

N2·8: Keigo II — Business Register Grammar

Business keigo builds on N3's honorific/humble basics with one more rule: 内・外 (uchi・soto). Speaking to an outside client, your own boss and coworkers become "your side" (内) — describe their actions with humble language, never honorific, even though you'd use honorific speech talking to that same boss directly.

1. 内 (uchi) vs 外 (soto) — who gets honored
  • To a coworker, inside the company: ぶちょうはもういらっしゃいました (buchou wa mou irasshaimashita — the manager has already arrived) — honorific, since everyone here is "inside."
  • To an outside client, about the very same manager: ぶちょうはもうまいりました (buchou wa mou mairimashita — the manager has already arrived) — humble, because from the client's view your manager is now "your side"; rank inside the company no longer matters.
  • This flip trips up learners more than any single verb form — the rule is about the listener, not the person being talked about.
2. ございます, おる, まいる — the business-formal set
  • です → でございます: こちらがしりょうでございます (kochira ga shiryou de gozaimasu — here is the material)
  • ある → ございます: おてあらいはあちらにございます (otearai wa achira ni gozaimasu — the restroom is over there)
  • いる → おる (humble, own side): たなかはただいまがいしゅつしております (Tanaka wa tadaima gaishutsu shite orimasu — Tanaka is currently out of the office)
  • いく／くる → まいる (humble, own side): すぐにまいります (sugu ni mairimasu — I'll be right there)
3. お／ご + stem + します — "let me do X for you"
  • お + verb ます-stem + します (or ご + verbal noun + します for many する-verbs) humbly offers to do something for the listener. Watch the honorific お doubling with an お-initial stem: おくる → おくり → おおくりします (okuru → okuri → o okuri shimasu — I will send it to you), not おくりします.
  • ごれんらくします (go renraku shimasu — I will contact you)
  • Standard phone/email openers and closers: いつもおせわになっております (itsumo osewa ni natte orimasu — thank you always for your support); よろしくおねがいいたします (yoroshiku onegai itashimasu — thank you in advance)

N1·1: Literary Negatives — ～んがため, ～ずして, ～べからず Grammar

N1 texts lean on classical grammar that survives only in set phrases, proverbs, and formal writing today. These three patterns all trace back to negatives or auxiliaries from classical Japanese — recognizing them unlocks mottos, signs, and literary prose.

1. ～んがため(に) — "in order to" (archaic purpose)
FormRuleExample
Regular verbsない-form, drop ない + んがために生きる → 生きない → 生きんがために (ikiru → ikinai → ikin ga tame ni — in order to live)
する (irregular)せんがために, never しんがために合格する → 合格せんがために (goukaku suru → goukaku sen ga tame ni — in order to pass [an exam])

Famous line: 人は生きんがために食う (hito wa ikin ga tame ni kuu — people eat in order to live) — the reverse of the usual "live to eat."

2. ～ずして — "without doing ~" (formal ～ないで)
  • ない-form, drop ない + ずして — する becomes せずして, never しずして.
  • 努力せずして成功はあり得ない (doryoku sezu shite seikou wa arienai — without effort, success is impossible)
  • Common in rhetorical questions built as "～ずして何であろうか" (sezu shite nan de arou ka — if this isn't ~, then what is?), affirming something emphatically by denying its alternative.
3. ～べからず — "must not" (formal prohibition)
  • Dictionary form + べからず — the negative of べし (must/should); する becomes すべからず, the one common irregular exception.
  • 芝生に入るべからず (shibafu ni hairu bekarazu — do not enter the lawn) — the classic wording on old signs and notices.
  • More common today as the plain notice 立入禁止, but べからず still appears on historical signs, in mottos, and in formal writing as a stronger, more literary alternative to ～べきではない.

N1·2: Concessive Nuance — ～ものを, ～ながら(も), ～といえども Grammar

Concessive grammar admits one fact while asserting something that seems to contradict it — "despite X, Y." These three patterns each add a different flavor: regret, quiet contrast, or formal argument.

1. ～ものを — regret: "if only..."
  • Attaches to a plain-form clause describing what should have happened — then leaves the speaker's frustration or regret hanging, since reality went otherwise.
  • すぐに謝ればいいものを、彼は意地を張った (sugu ni ayamareba ii mono o, kare wa iji o hatta — he should have just apologized right away, but he stubbornly refused)
  • Often the clause after ものを is dropped entirely, leaving the regret implied: 早く言ってくれればよかったものを…(hayaku itte kurereba yokatta mono o… — if only you'd told me sooner…)
2. ～ながら(も) — "even though / despite"
  • い-adjective / な-adjective stem (no な, e.g. 残念ながら) / noun / verb ます-stem + ながら(も) — different from the simultaneous-action ながら from N4; here it concedes a contrast within a single subject.
  • 狭いながらも、幸せな家庭を築いた (semai nagara mo, shiawase na katei o kizuita — despite being small, [they] built a happy home)
  • 知っていながら、彼は黙っていた (shitte inagara, kare wa damatte ita — even though he knew, he stayed silent).
3. ～といえども — "even ~ / even though" (formal)
  • Noun / plain form + といえども — grants a point fully, then insists it changes nothing; common in essays, speeches, and legal-style argument.
  • 子供といえども、責任は免れない (kodomo to iedomo, sekinin wa manugarenai — even though [he's] a child, [he] cannot escape responsibility)
  • プロといえども、失敗することはある (puro to iedomo, shippai suru koto wa aru — even professionals sometimes fail).

N1·3: Immediacy — ～なり, ～や否や, ～そばから Grammar

Three ways to say "the instant X happens, Y follows" — but they aren't interchangeable. なり and や否や describe a single unexpected moment; そばから describes a frustrating cycle that keeps repeating.

1. ～なり — "as soon as" (a single surprising follow-up)
  • Dictionary form + なり — the second action follows the first almost instantly, and is often unexpected; the subject of both clauses is normally the same person.
  • 彼は帰宅するなり、部屋に閉じこもった (kare wa kitaku suru nari, heya ni tojikomotta — as soon as he got home, he shut himself in his room)
2. ～や否や(やいなや) — "the moment that..." (literary)
  • Dictionary form + や否や — more literary than なり, common in narrative writing; the two clauses can involve different subjects.
  • ベルが鳴るや否や、生徒たちは教室を飛び出した (beru ga naru ya ina ya, seitotachi wa kyoushitsu o tobidashita — the moment the bell rang, the students dashed out of the classroom)
3. ～そばから — "no sooner...than" (a repeating cycle)
  • Dictionary form / た-form + そばから — unlike なり and や否や, this describes a repeated, often frustrating pattern where the second action keeps undoing the first.
  • 片付けるそばから、子供が散らかす (katazukeru soba kara, kodomo ga chirakasu — no sooner do I tidy up than the kids make a mess again)
  • 習ったそばから忘れてしまう (naratta soba kara wasurete shimau — I forget things as fast as I learn them).

N1·4: Formal Circumstance — ～ともなると, ～とあって, ～に至って Grammar

These three describe how a special stage, occasion, or extreme point changes everything around it — standard fare in news reports and formal commentary.

1. ～ともなると／～ともなれば — "once it reaches the stage of..."
  • Noun / verb dictionary form + ともなると(なれば) — a special level, time, or status is reached, and things naturally follow from it.
  • 大企業の社長ともなると、責任は非常に重い (dai kigyou no shachou tomo naru to, sekinin wa hijou ni omoi — when one becomes the president of a major company, the responsibility is extremely heavy)
  • 年末ともなれば、街は買い物客でにぎわう (nenmatsu tomo nareba, machi wa kaimonokyaku de nigiwau — when it's the end of the year, the streets bustle with shoppers).
2. ～とあって — "given that it's a special ~"
  • Noun / plain form + とあって — states a special circumstance, then describes the natural reaction it causes; common in news-style writing.
  • 連休とあって、高速道路はどこも渋滞している (renkyuu to atte, kousoku douro wa doko mo juutai shiteiru — since it's a long holiday, the highways are congested everywhere)
3. ～に至って(いたって) — "only when it reached the point of..."
  • Noun / verb dictionary form + に至って — something is finally realized, decided, or acted on only once an extreme point is reached, often implying "too late."
  • 手術が必要な状態に至って、彼はやっと病院へ行った (shujutsu ga hitsuyou na joutai ni itatte, kare wa yatto byouin e itta — only when it reached the point of needing surgery did he finally go to the hospital)
  • Related: ～に至っては singles out one extreme example within a larger group for comment; ～に至るまで means "all the way up to ~."

N1·5: Character & Set Phrases — ～きらいがある, ～ずにはおかない, ～を余儀なくされる Grammar

Three set phrases for describing tendencies and forced outcomes — a person's negative habit, an inevitable emotional effect, and a circumstance nobody chose.

1. ～きらいがある — "has a tendency to ~" (negative)
  • Verb dictionary form + きらいがある, or noun + の + きらいがある — describes an inherent, usually undesirable tendency or bad habit, not a one-time event.
  • 彼は仕事を先延ばしにするきらいがある (kare wa shigoto o sakinobashi ni suru kirai ga aru — he has a tendency to procrastinate on work)
2. ～ないではおかない／～ずにはおかない — "bound to ~ / cannot help but ~"
  • ない-form + ではおかない, or ない-form (drop ない) + ずにはおかない — a natural force or strong emotion makes an effect inevitable; the double negative reads as a strong positive.
  • この映画は観客を感動させないではおかない (kono eiga wa kankyaku o kandou sasenai de wa okanai — this movie is bound to move the audience)
  • 彼の行動は皆を怒らせずにはおかない (kare no koudou wa mina o okorasezu niwa okanai — his behavior can't help but anger everyone).
3. ～を余儀なくされる(よぎなくされる) — "be forced to ~"
  • Noun (often a verb turned into a noun) + を余儀なくされる — circumstances beyond anyone's control force an unwanted outcome; a formal, passive-feeling construction common in news reports.
  • 台風のため、試合は中止を余儀なくされた (taifuu no tame, shiai wa chuushi o yoginaku sareta — due to the typhoon, the match was forced to be canceled)

N1·6: Reading the Editorial Grammar

N1 reading comprehension leans heavily on 論説文 — opinion essays and newspaper editorials. Knowing their stock phrases and connectors turns a wall of formal text into a predictable argument structure. Two keigo traps round out this lesson.

1. Stock editorial phrases (論説文の決まり文句)
  • ～と言わざるを得ない (to iwazaru o enai — cannot help but say/conclude that): この結果は失敗だったと言わざるを得ない (kono kekka wa shippai datta to iwazaru o enai — one cannot help but conclude this result was a failure)
  • ～ではなかろうか (de wa nakarou ka — isn't it perhaps...?): a softened, formal way to state an opinion as a rhetorical question. それは杞憂ではなかろうか (sore wa kiyuu de wa nakarou ka — isn't that perhaps needless worry?)
  • ～に他ならない (ni hoka naranai — is nothing other than): 彼の成功は努力の賜物に他ならない (kare no seikou wa doryoku no tamamono ni hoka naranai — his success is nothing other than the fruit of effort)
2. Formal connectors — したがって, もっとも, ゆえに
  • したがって (shitagatte — therefore, accordingly): links a conclusion drawn logically from what came before, more formal than だから.
  • もっとも (mottomo — that said, although): follows a claim with a qualification or exception, softening what was just argued.
  • ゆえに (yueni — therefore, hence): the most formal/logical of the three, common in academic and legal writing; often preceded by それ (それゆえに).
3. Two keigo traps
  • 二重敬語 (double keigo): おっしゃられる is wrong — it stacks the already-honorific おっしゃる (say) with the honorific suffix ～られる. Say just おっしゃる, or 言われる.
  • 伺う vs 参る (both humble "go/come," but not interchangeable): 伺う (ukagau) implies going to see, ask, or hear from someone of higher status — directed at the other party. 参る (mairu) is a plain humble "go/come" with no such direction, fine for general business use: 今参ります (ima mairimasu — I'm coming right now).
